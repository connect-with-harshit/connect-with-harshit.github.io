I.EVO · Bespoke Furniture · Udaipur

Rebuilding Operations from the Inside

Feb 2024 – Aug 2025 · 18 months
The Situation

I.EVO is a bespoke furniture manufacturer — every project custom, every client different. But the team had developed a costly habit: treating every product as if it had never been made before.

Each new enquiry triggered a full R&D cycle — drawings, material selection, fabric selection, finish decisions, mock-up production — even for items already designed, sampled, and delivered on previous projects. Files weren't maintained. Institutional knowledge walked out with each completed project.

The result: even loose furniture or small-quantity orders inherited the same 180-day timeline as a 100-room hospitality fit-out. Clients were demotivated. Deals were being lost before they started.

What I Built

Within weeks of joining, I flagged the pattern to management and got alignment to build a Standard Product Library — a structured repository of every item I.EVO had previously designed, sampled, and produced, complete with all associated production files.

The library served two purposes simultaneously. Internally, it eliminated repeated R&D effort. Externally, it became a client tool — physical samples were catalogued so clients could touch, feel, and evaluate finished products before committing.

For clients selecting from the library: no mock-up phase, no material uncertainty, no approval loops. Files were ready. Samples were available. Production could begin immediately.

The Pushback

The idea wasn't welcomed at first. Both the owner and the head of production pushed back, worried that reusing designs across different hospitality brands would create "design fatigue" — clients feeling like they were getting someone else's product. The design head went further, directly questioning whether I understood the industry well enough to make the call.

I didn't argue the point. Instead, I brought data: international hospitality chains typically refresh their design dockets every 2–3 years anyway, because they hit the same fatigue internally. The fix wasn't to hide the library — it was to position it as an option alongside a brand's own docket, not a replacement for it. If a client picked something from the library, we could replicate it at a fraction of the cost and time since the R&D was already done.

That reframing landed. I got the green light to build it properly, and the library grew to over 1,000 physical products with full production-ready documentation behind each one.

180→45

Days to delivery for standard product orders — R&D, material selection, and mock-up phases eliminated entirely

~70%

Reduction in PM team effort per standard project — same team, significantly higher throughput

↑

Smaller projects and loose furniture became commercially viable — deals no longer lost to timeline friction

↑

Client experience transformed — physical samples enabled confident, faster decisions at appraisal

The library didn't compromise I.EVO's bespoke positioning — full custom R&D remained available for clients who needed it. It simply gave the business a second gear: a faster, lower-friction path for clients who valued speed and certainty over complete customisation.
Arten Space · Bespoke Furniture Manufacturer · Udaipur

Building an Operating System from the Ground Up

Dec 2025 – Present · Ongoing
The Situation

Arten Space had the right ingredients — quality product, strong design sensibility, and genuine relationships with architects, interior designers, and HNWI clients across Udaipur and PAN India. Business was flowing. But underneath, the operating system was failing quietly.

The brand is led by two co-founders, both of whom needed visibility into operations without being pulled into the daily weeds. That meant building one system that could keep both informed and aligned — not two separate reporting tracks that risked diverging.

Every department ran on paper coupons — material requisitions, gate passes, job cards, petty cash, dispatch tracking. Data entry executives sat at every process node, manually transcribing and re-entering data. Despite all this effort, nothing was actually visible. Management had no live picture of what was happening on the floor, in the store, or in accounts.

There was no leave policy. No productivity measurement. No QC at department level. Rework was common but unquantified. HR was drowning in daily paperwork. Broken child processes upstream of production — compounded by poor cross-team communication — were silently costing the business every day.

What I Built

I started with a ground-level RCA — learning every process in detail, including the smallest decisions, before proposing anything. Then rebuilt systematically across four areas:

Coupon System → Google Sheets. Eliminated the entire paper coupon infrastructure across production, accounts, store, dispatch, and installation. Replaced with structured Google Sheets — binary data entry for ground-level staff, cell-edit restrictions by role. One change centralised accounts, purchase, store, and production into one visible system — and freed every data entry executive from manual transcription.

QC Framework. Introduced product-level quality checks at both department and dispatch stages — random sampling, structured sign-off, documented rework triggers. Previously invisible rework costs became measurable and reducible — worth up to 30% of original production cost.

Productivity Tracking. Built a supervisor-level data capture system feeding into a comparative productivity matrix — giving management the first real visibility into output per department, per day.

HRMS + Leave Policy. Implemented a low-cost HRMS accessible to non-tech-savvy floor staff for self-managing attendance, overtime, and leave regularisation. Introduced the company's first formal leave policy alongside it. Daily HR paperwork dropped by 80%.

~90%

Reduction in manual reconciliation — integrated store, inventory & accounts system replaced daily coupon transcription

180 hrs

Saved per month — data entry executives redeployed to audits and higher-value operational work

~30%

Production cost savings via QC — rework at department and dispatch level quantified and eliminated

80%

Reduction in daily HR paperwork — HRMS + leave policy automated attendance, payslips & leave management

The deeper win wasn't any single system — it was that for the first time, management could see the business clearly. Live data across production, store, accounts, and HR meant decisions could be made on facts rather than instinct. The operating system finally matched the quality of the product.

The real questions

Things you were going to ask.

The questions every recruiter has but rarely gets honest answers to. Here are mine.

Where do you see yourself in five years?

Honestly? I don't have a fixed destination mapped out — and I think anyone who claims otherwise in 2026 isn't being fully truthful about how fast things are changing.

What I do know is the shape of where I'm heading: leading a product, program, or delivery team at meaningful scale — the kind of impact I'm building right now, but bigger. And doing it in a way where the people around me grow alongside me. I've always believed that a leader who doesn't bring people up with them is just someone who got there first.

The destination might shift. The direction won't.

What are your strengths — and be honest about the weakness too.

Strength: I can get to the root of a problem faster than most — not because I'm particularly clever, but because I watch carefully and I put myself genuinely in the other person's position before I open my mouth. That goes for client issues, team issues, operational breakdowns. I don't recommend a fix until I actually understand what the person in front of me is experiencing.

Weakness — the real one, not the rehearsed one: that same empathy can make me a difficult employee sometimes. If I believe a client or a vendor or a team member is genuinely being wronged, I'll say so to my employer. Even when it's uncomfortable. I've gone to bat for the other side more than once when I felt the situation called for it.

Some people see that as a problem. I see it as the cost of operating with integrity. Make of that what you will.

Why are you looking to move on?

I'm not unhappy. But I'm not being fully used either.

I've worked across logistics, retail, manufacturing, project delivery, and general management. I've built systems from scratch, rebuilt broken ones, and handed off functioning operations to teams I trained. And right now I can feel the gap between what I'm doing and what I'm capable of.

I'm ambitious — not in a restless way, but in the way where staying still when you could be moving forward feels like a waste. The right opportunity hasn't been about money or title for a long time. It's about scale, complexity, and whether the problem in front of me is actually worth solving.

I'm looking because I'm ready for the next version of the problem.

What do you do outside of work?

Most of my time right now goes to my daughter. That's not a line — she's genuinely the majority of my non-working hours and I wouldn't have it any other way.

Beyond that, I socialise deliberately. I find that talking to people across different industries is one of the fastest ways to understand what's actually happening in the market — not what LinkedIn says is happening, but what business owners and operators are actually dealing with on the ground. I collect those data points.

I also have a habit of deconstructing businesses I encounter — how did they get here, what decisions led to their current shape, where is the pain point they haven't solved yet. It's part observation, part entertainment. That habit makes me a sharper operator inside other people's businesses — and I think it always will.

Why you, over another operations manager?

I haven't gone a single working day without being employed or productive since I was 15. I come from a family of jewelry manufacturers — the kind of family where children grow up watching their parents work 18-hour days, not just for income, but because work is how they give their life meaning. That's the only version of work I've ever known.

I don't operate like an employee. I operate like someone who will be accountable for the outcome — because in my head, if I fail, the business fails. That's not a motivational poster. It's just how I'm wired.

And I don't just manage myself that way. I've consistently brought that out in people around me too. Not through pressure — through the kind of environment where doing good work feels like the natural thing to do.

Most operations managers manage processes. I take ownership of outcomes. That's the difference.

What kind of environment brings out your best work?

Collaborative and ownership-driven. Those two things together.

I've worked in a lot of environments. The ones that drain me the fastest are where people treat showing up as the deliverable — where "I'm just an employee" is said without embarrassment, and where ownership is something that gets passed around like a hot potato.

The environments where I do my best work are where people actually care — where someone on the floor takes pride in a finished product, where a manager doesn't need to be chased for a decision, where the team collectively acts like the business is theirs to protect. I'm not looking for perfection. I'm looking for intent.

Give me that environment and I'll give you everything I have.

Tell me about a project that didn't go the way you wanted.

At Spinny, right after a major funding round, every city team was racing to hit a national delivery milestone first. I was simultaneously asked to build a new program from scratch — delivering cars via flatbed trucks instead of standard home delivery, for a more premium client experience.

I flagged it early: the unit economics didn't work. Truck deliveries cost roughly 20x a home delivery and 40x a hub delivery. I said so clearly, more than once. Management pushed forward anyway, the business case was about visible fund utilisation more than long-term sustainability. We complied. Within months, 60% of all home deliveries were running on trucks, and our vendor had scaled their fleet from 20 trucks to over 70, custom-built to our brand spec, on the strength of the volume we'd shown them.

When the cost data finally piled up and the margin damage became undeniable, the program was shut down overnight. We lost the vendor relationship, the fleet investment, client delivery commitments, and a fair amount of credibility with leadership, even though I'd flagged the risk from day one.

The personal cost was real, even for a decision I didn't make but helped execute. What I took from it: being right early isn't the same as being heard early, and the way you handle the aftermath of someone else's bad call matters more than being able to say "I told you so." I made sure the vendor was treated fairly in the wind-down and that the data trail existed so the same mistake wouldn't repeat. That's the part I actually control.
